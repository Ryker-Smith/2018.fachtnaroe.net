2.2 Enhancing the basic network

Many small businesses, initially happy with the benefits of their 10Mbps Ethernet LAN, may notice that the network's previously robust performance declines as conditions in the office change due to:

  • The addition of new users to the network.
  • The use of new applications that involve bigger files.
  • The move to a client/server environment that may cause a potential " bottleneck" at the network server.

Any of these situations can make the network less responsive, annoying users with perceived sluggishness, and reducing worker productivity. An incremental, standards–based migration strategy to 100Mbps Fast Ethernet is the solution. Fast Ethernet is ten times faster than 10Mbps Ethernet at far less than ten times the price.

3 Connecting to the Internet

3.1 Business benefits of the Internet

The Internet has developed over the past few years into an indispensable resource for communication and commerce. Even the very smallest businesses can use the Internet to increase profitability and productivity. Some of the benefits include:

  • E-mail communications and file transfers to partners, suppliers, and customers around the globe, 24-hours a day.
  • Web access to information resources about market trends, competitors, and other business research.
  • Expanded sales opportunities using the Web to market your business via a basic Web site implemented by your Internet Service Provider (ISP). Your business will, in that way, be more "visible" to the outside world.
  • Improved customer relations through on-line ordering and other customer services (often referred to as "e-commerce").

3.2 Choosing between single user and shared Internet access

The linking of your office location to the Internet can be accomplished in one of two ways:

1. Stand-alone connections - these use a dial-up modem attached to an individual PC. This solution relies on software in the PC to handle the details of addressing the Internet. Note that only one person at a time can access the Internet this way. Other users must wait for the modem-equipped PC to become free.

2. Shared connections - allow multiple users to access the Internet simultaneously. These highly efficient shared connections rely on a device called a router or a LAN Modem, which combines the functions of a modem with powerful Internet addressing software. Because the access router attaches to the LAN, not to an individual PC, it is available to several users on the LAN at any time (25 users in the case of an OfficeConnect LAN Modem).

3.3 Benefits of shared Internet access

For the small business LAN, shared Internet connections are more economical and manageable than, say, equipping users with their own stand-alone modems and phone lines. Benefits of shared Internet connections include:

  • Lower operational costs – a single ISP account and telephone line can service many users simultaneously.
  • Lower capital costs – one access device is more economical than single-user modems.
  • Easily expanded – as new users join the LAN, they receive Internet access automatically.

3.4 Securing the Internet Connection

While the Internet is an amazing resource, Internet users need to protect themselves from potential dangers. Some experts say that anyone connected to the Internet is at risk from Internet attacks. Your risk increases the more time you spend connected. For instance, a broadband connection like cable or DSL is "always on" meaning that it is connected to the Internet 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. By placing a firewall device between your LAN (switch or hub) and the Internet Access device (router, modem, or terminal adapter) you can protect your network. Firewalls protect a network and allow users to control and monitor Internet use.

4 Connecting to the remote office

4.1 What is Access Routing?

"Dial-up routing", also known as "access routing" or "remote access", is the technology to connect two geographically separate LANs using public telephone lines. Dial-up routing makes users on each LAN feel as if they are part of one, larger LAN. Dial-up routing also includes special features that minimize the traffic to be transferred over the dial-up line, increasing performance while reducing phone charges.

The networking devices that perform these routing functions are called "access routers". Access routers are the key to linking together the various separate offices that may make up your business. Access routing makes a small business more powerful by "expanding" company borders to encompass any location where a small LAN is installed. You get the benefits of "big-company" integration with "small-company" simplicity and economy.

4.2 What are the key benefits of connecting Remote Offices?

  • Branch offices appear as if they were permanently attached to the headquarters LAN, facilitating communications and workplace collaboration of all kinds.
  • In contrast to stand-alone, single-PC remote access solutions using modems and dedicated phone lines, every user in the remote office can easily access the remote LAN without the need to install multiple phone lines and modems.
  • Transmitting electronic documents over the network reduces delivery costs compared to faxing or land-based courier and mail systems; using e-mail reduces long distance telephone charges.

4.3 How does Access Routing work?

To connect two remote LANs together, each LAN must have an access router (such as an OfficeConnect Remote product).

The access routers "talk" to each other over the access link, such as an analog V.90 or digital ISDN connection, and forward the communications traffic received onto the local LAN and to the PCs via NICs.

The two access routers establish the LAN-to-LAN connection "transparently," without users even being aware it is happening. This is because access routers implement "dial-on-demand," which means that the connection is "brought up" (dialed and activated) only when there is network data to transmit. Dial-on-demand helps to keep toll charges low by eliminating the time that links are active but without traffic.

5 Network Security and Firewalls

5.1 What is a Firewall?

A firewall is a network security device that protects your network and allows the user to control Internet use. It monitors and examines all of the data traffic flowing between the network and the Internet, allowing only authorized users into the network and access to only approved portions of the Internet. In this way a firewall blocks hacker and Denial of Service attacks, protecting the files, records and data on the network, and ensures that the Internet is used as the network administrator intended. The OfficeConnect Internet Firewalls provide small networks with "large enterprise" protection, using Stateful Packet Inspection filtering. 3Com's Internet Firewalls have also been certified by the ICSA (International Computer Security Association), the industry's authority on Internet and computer security.

6 Wireless Networking

6.1 What are the benefits of wireless networking?

The benefits of a wireless network include:

  • Fast, simple installation.
  • Flexible configuration.
  • Easy add-on for new workstations and devices.
  • Increased mobility in the workplace.
  • Portability for the network itself.Low total cost of ownership.

Existing Ethernet networks also benefit from wireless technology. Sites which have 10BASE-T or 100BASE-TX networking already installed can extend the cabled network by connecting an access point to the existing network, adding new users at reduced cost as well as providing mobility for notebook computer users.

While WLANs can have advantages in terms of flexibility and mobility, wireless links are more sensitive to environmental considerations. Often, adjusting the antenna on the access point or reorienting the wireless client clears local interference. If you require increased coverage of the service area, simply install additional access points.

6.2 A simple wireless network

The simplest wireless topology consists of two wireless clients (for example, a notebook computer and a desktop computer) communicating with each other on a peer-to-peer network. If a more complex network is required, a single access point can be installed to provide a single-cell network for up to 63 wireless clients.

In the following figure:

  • The access point serves the same purpose as a stand-alone network hub and is not connected to any other network segments. All the desktop and laptop PCs in the coverage area are linked to the wireless network. They can be moved anywhere within the coverage area and still maintain their network links.
  • Shared peripheral devices are linked to the network through individual PCs, and so access privileges must be granted to the rest of the users.
  • The LAN Modem provides Internet access for users in the access point's domain.

6.3 Separate networks at one site

Multiple access points can coexist as separate networks at the same site without interference using different network identifiers (WLAN service areas). In addition, wireless clients can be associated with an access point to enable network connections anywhere within the coverage area. The wireless clients can move within the coverage area of one access point and remain connected, or can be configured to roam to the coverage area of a different access point, and communicate with the wireless clients associated with that access point.

In the following figure:

  • A single Ethernet backbone runs through the office suite.
  • Peripheral devices are connected to the Ethernet backbone through a hub or a switch.
  • A router provides Ethernet access for all users to other subnetworks.
  • Each access point can be configured as a separate subnetwork to communicate only with wireless clients associated with that subnetwork.
  • Optionally, the access points can be configured to let mobile users roam between coverage areas without losing their current network link.

6.4 Wireless extension to existing ethernet

Another possible wireless topology is a single access point bridging an Ethernet and wireless network. The access point, wired to a network server or LAN through an Ethernet cable, serves as a network node and provides the link between the server and the wireless clients. The wireless clients can move freely throughout the coverage area of the access point while remaining connected to the server.

In the following figure:

  • Existing computers and peripheral devices are connected to the Ethernet backbone.
  • When the network expands, an access point is connected through a switch to the Ethernet backbone.
  • New users access the network through the access point.

6.5 Wireless strategy as part of an integrated network

If you are planning a new network installation, consider using wireless and wired local area networking together for a low-cost, extremely versatile, integrated network. Individual access points can be configured to provide basic service sets of wireless clients. These basic service sets might consist of fixed groups of users, office equipment, or independent subnets differentiated by corporate function. The wired Ethernet backbone can serve as the core data distribution system tying all of basic service sets together into a single extended service set. If you require mobility, or simply the convenience of staying connected by notebook computer to a particular server, you can roam across access point boundaries as you move about the network site.

In the following figure:

  • The network has been planned around the strategic installation of a single Ethernet backbone.
  • Key peripheral devices are connected to the Ethernet backbone through the switch.
  • Desktop and laptop computers are linked to the Ethernet backbone through the wireless network.
  • Optionally, peripherals can be accessed from the network through a PC.
  • Mobile users can roam across the entire site, always within the coverage area of an access point and always connected to the network.
