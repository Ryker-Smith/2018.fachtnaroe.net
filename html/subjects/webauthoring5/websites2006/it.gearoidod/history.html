History and Structure of The Internet

Where did the Internet come from?


The Internet has revolutionised how many people communicate. It allows people to send messages and files to each other, world wide, in a matter of seconds. It is the fastest and one of the most commonly used communication tools in the world. It also covers the widest area and does so inexpensively.


The Internet started in the late 1960s as project of the U.S. Department of Defense: the military needed a communications network that would function even if the parts of the network were broken. They also needed a network that could work between machines of different types.


In October 1969 two computers “talked” for the first time across a telephone line. This network was known as ARPAnet. As time went on ARPAnet grew and incorporated many more systems. As the network quickly expanded academics and researchers in other fields began to use it as well.


Internet Protocol (IP) was developed in order to get different computers to communicate through a network. Internet Protocol is a set of laws which any computers belonging to a network must follow.


The first email programme was developed in 1972 by Ray Tomlinson of BBN. The Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) was renamed the Defense Research Projects Agency (DARPA) in this year as well.


1n 1973 development of the protocol TCP/IP began. It was developed by a group headed by Vinton Cerf (Stanford) and Bob Kahn (DARPA).


Dr. R M Metcalfe developed a system which allowed coaxial cable to move data extremely fast in 1976. This system was called Ethernet. In this year the packet satellite project (SATNET) went into practical use. The Department of Defense began to experiment with th TCP/IP protocol and decided to require it for use on ARPANET. A year later UUCP developed at AT&T Bell Labs. This was later used in conjunction with UNIX.


In 1979 the decentralized news group network (USENET) was developed by Steve Bellovin, Tom Truscott and Jim Ellis. The Creation of BITNET, by IBM, "Because its Time Network", introduced the "store and forward" network. It was used for email and listservs.


2 years later the National Science Foundation created backbone called CSNET for institutions without access to ARPANET. Vinton Cerf proposed a link between APRANET and CSNET.


On January 1st of 1983 every computer connected to ARPANET had to use TCP/IP. This became the standard protocol and completely replaced NCP.


The University of Wisconsin also created the DNS (Domain Name System) in this year as well.This allowed packets to be directed to a domain name, which would be translated by the server database into the corresponding IP number.


In 1984 the ARPANET was divided into two networks: MILNET and ARPANET. MILNET was made to support the military's needs while ARPANET supported the research component. The Department of Defense continued to support both networks.


Also in this year and upgrade to CSNET was contracted by MCI. New circuits would be T1 lines, i.5 Mbps which is twenty five times than the old 56 Kbps lines. IBM would provide advanced routers and Merit would manage the network


In 1990 Merit, IBM and MCI formed a not for profit corporation called ANS, Advanced Network & Services, which was to conduct research into high speed networking. It soon came up with the concept of the T3, a 45 Mbps line. NSF quickly adopted the new network and by the end of 1991 all of its sites were connected by this new backbone.


CSNET was discounted in 1991 after having fulfilled its important role in the provision of academic network servicing.


Also this year the NSF established a new network, named NREN, the National Research and Education Network. The purpose of this network is to conduct high speed networking research. It was not to be used as a commercial network, nor was it to be used tosend a lot of the data that the Internet now transfers.
