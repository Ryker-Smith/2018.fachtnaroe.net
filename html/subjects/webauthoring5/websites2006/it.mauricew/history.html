Where Did The Internet Come From?


The internet was first thought of in the year 1962. Paul Baran of the RAND corporation(a government agency) was commissioned by the government agency to try find a way that they could maintain control of their missiles and weaponry after a nuclear attack. Baran's finished document contained several suggestions on how to do this. His final idea was a packet switched network. A packet switched network is defined as: "Packet switching is the breaking down of data into datagrams or packets that are labeled to indicate the origin and the destination of the information and the forwarding of these packets from one computer to another computer until the information arrives at its final destination computer. This was crucial to the realization of a computer network. If packets are lost at any given point, the message can be resent by the originator."


6 years later in 1968, the idea was realised when ARPA awarded the ARPANET contract to BBN. BBN had selected a Honeywell minicomputer as the base on which they would build the switch. Four nodes were linked from which the switch would run. The four nods would be located at University of Califor nia(Los Angeles), SRI in Stanford, University of California(Santa Barbara) and University of Utah.


In 1972, a huge step was taken in making the internet what it is today. Ray Tomlinson created the first e-mail program. The Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) was renamed The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (or DARPA). ARPANET was currently using the Network Control Protocol or NCP to transfer data. This allowed communications between hosts running on the same network.


In 1973, Development began on the protocol later to be called TCP/IP, it was developed by a group headed by Vinton Cerf from Stanford and Bob Kahn from DARPA. This new protocol was to allow computer networks to interconnect and communicate with each other.

In 1976 the ethrenet was developed. One year later the UUCP (Unix-to-Unix CoPy) developed at AT&T Bell Labs and was later used in conjunction with UNIX.


In 1983, the IAB(Internet Activities Board) was created. On January 1st of that year, every computer network connected to ARPANET had to TCP/IP. It became the core internet protocol.


Continuous developments occurred until 1992 when the greatest advancement happened. This was the world wide web(www.), which was released by CERN.

By 1994, many new networks had been added to the NSF backbone. Hundred's of thousand's of new users were added to the INTERNET by this time.

However, this leads on to other questions. One of which is who owns the internet. The answer is not owned by anyone in particular. More of a concept than an actual tangible entity, the Internet relies on a physical infrastructure that connects one network to other networks. There are many organisations, corporations, governments, schools, private citizens and service providers that all own pieces of the infrastructure, but there is no one body that owns it all. However. Some organisations control the running and smooth operation of the internet. This includes assigning IP addresses and domain names.
