The Internet


History & Structure of The Internet


Who owns the internet?


In theory, everyone who uses the internet, owns the internet. However, in practice, although no one person, people or organization owns the internet, there are some organizations who own their 'pieces' of the internet and exert their influence over it, for example; The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (Icann) under contract to the US department of commerce.


The ICANN is based in California in the United States of America. As the US were the initial developers of the internet, they see it as their responsibility to oversee and manage it. They do this however, without directly interfering in the running of it.


This has caused conflicts with several other nations including the EU who want a more hands on approach to the internet, and the UN who want to enforce the internet themselves. This is the complete opposite to the United States's standpoint that; the internet provides a freedom that should not and will not be taken away.


Another noteworthy point to add, is that even though nobody actually owns the internet, there are still laws applicable to it that must be obeyed; as if you break a law on the internet, it is treated the same as if you broke a law on the street of a city.


Where did the internet come from?


The internet was originally developed in the sixties by the US government who were caught in a panic of being left behind technologically when Sputnik, a Soviet satellite was launched into space in October 1957. The idea was that even if one computer is hit, the rest of the network would still be able to function effectively. It was developed by the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) and was originally called the ARPANET.


ARPA was created by President Dwight Eisenhower. It was created by a group of scientists for use in the United States military however, over the years it was made available for public use and now anyone can get access.


Where did the world wide web come from?


The world wide web was created by Tim Berners Lee, a British computer scientist who was living in Switzerland at the time, working in a physics laboratory. He was dissatisfied with the disorder and difficult task at hand ever time he tried to locate a file. He wanted an easier way around it. And so, fed up, he created a hypertext program that allowed him to link the files from different databases with ease.


Soon enough, he had the program expand beyond the laboratory where he was working and allowed it to link different databases all over the world together. He called this the World Wide Web. Not long afterwards, this idea was picked up by several organizations who began to make it their own. Some students from the University of Illinois took this program and with it they created Mosaic, the first web browser, turning Lee's solely textual program into a point-and-click graphical one.

Can I break the internet?


A dangerous virus can be sent which can infect millions upon millions of users' computers, however not all machines will be infected, on top of that, the internet itself cannot be broken as it is essentially a network of networks. For the same reason the internet was established in the first place, is the same reason why it cannot be broken today; even if you hit one (or many) of the computers, the rest will still function and the internet as a whole will remain intact.
