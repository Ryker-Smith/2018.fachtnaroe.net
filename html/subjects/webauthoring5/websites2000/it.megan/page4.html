How do cancers spread?

Cancers are not infections and do not spread from person to person.  However, once they advance beyond a certain point, they become able to spread within a person.  A tumour that has the potential to spread is malignant because it has more serious implications for the patient.  Cancers spread by invading the surrounding tissue until they reach a blood or lymph vessel.  Small groups of cells may then break off from the original tumour (primary tumour).  These are then carried via the bloodstream to other parts of the body, where they may settle and grow.  This process is called metastasising.  The new cancers that develop are known as secondary cancers.
 


Tumours

> Benign tumours:
> are not cancerous
> can usually be removed
> do not come back in most cases
> do not spread to other parts of the body and the cells do not invade other tissues
> Malignant tumours:
> are cancerous
> can invade and damage nearby tissues and organs
> metastasise (cancer cells break away from a malignant tumour and enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system to form secondary tumours in other parts of the body)
