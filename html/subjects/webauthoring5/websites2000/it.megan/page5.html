Treatment



The three main types of treatment for cancer are surgery, chemotherapy and radiotherapy.  If someone has been diagnosed with cancer, the treatment that they would receive would depend on the type of cancer and whether it had spread.  Sometimes people are given a combination of two or more types of treatment.
 

Surgery

This is an operation to remove the cancer.  Surgery is often combined with other types of treatment to make sure all the cancer cells are killed.

Radiotherapy

This means using specialised machines to direct beams of radiation at the area of the body where the cancer cells are.  The radiation kills or inactivates the cancer cells so they stop dividing and the tumour stops growing.  Sometimes radiotherapy is used to shrink the tumour so that it is easier to remove by surgery.

External: Beam that is directed at an angle to penetrate the affected area but reduce the amount of exposure to any one area of the skin.
>  Marks on skin to ensure the patient is positioned properly for each treatment.
> Actual treatment lasts for 2 - 5 minutes.

Internal: Implantation - radioisotope in a metal case cover e.g. needles, seeds, planted in a cavity or implanted in tissue during an operation.
 

Chemotherapy

This means treating the cancer cells with drugs that are designed to kill them.  The drugs are carried around in the bloodstream and so can reach different areas in the body at the same time.  For instance, it is an effective treatment for leukaemia, cancer of the blood cells, where the disease is present through the body.

Chemotherapy may offer a cure, provide long term control and improved survival and palliation.  It may be used as a primary form of treatment or in combination with surgery or radiation, this is known as adjutant chemotherapy.

Palliative chemotherapy: This may be used as treatment for people whose cancer cannot be controlled or cured but where it would be of benefit to the person to control pain and other distressing symptoms.
