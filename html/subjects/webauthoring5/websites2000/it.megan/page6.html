Lung cancer




Lung cancer is cancer that usually starts in the lining of the bronchi, but can also begin in other areas of the respiratory system, including the trachea, bronchioles, or alveoli.
Lung cancers are believed to develop over a period of many years.
 

Causes of Lung Cancer

> Smoking - risk increased with number and type of cigarettes smoked.
> Passive Smoking - increased risk up to 30% in non-smokers.
> Long term exposure to chemicals and substances - asbestos, uranium,   chromium, nickel, asphalt and tar.
 

What are the risk factors for lung cancer?

A risk factor is anything that increases a person's chance of getting a disease such as cancer.  Different cancers have different risk factors.  Several risk factors make a person more likely to develop lung cancer:
 

  • People who start to smoke in adolescence and who continue to smoke as adults are at the greatest risk of developing lung cancer in adult life.
  • The more cigarettes you smoke the greater the risk.
  • Pipe and cigar smokers have a much greater risk of developing lung cancer than non-smokers.


Smoking marijuana cigarettes, which:

> Contain more tar than tobacco cigarettes.
> Are inhaled very deeply.
> Are smoked all the way to the end where tar content is the highest.

Signs and symptoms of lung cancer

The following are the most common symptoms for lung cancer; however, each individual may experience symptoms differently.
Lung cancer usually does not cause symptoms when it first develops, but they often become present after the tumour begins growing.  A persistent cough or hoarseness is the most common symptom of lung cancer.  Other symptoms include:

  • constant chest pain
  • shortness of breath
  • wheezing
  • recurring lung infections, such as pneumonia or bronchitis
  • bloody or rust coloured sputum
  • a tumour that presses on large blood vessels near the lung can cause swelling of the neck and face
  • a tumour that presses on certain nerves near the lung causing pain and weakness in the shoulder, arm, or hand
  • fever for unknown reason
  • fatigue, loss of appetite and loss of weight
Diagnosis for lung cancer

In addition to a complete medical history to check for risk factors and symptoms, and a physical examination to provide other information about signs of lung cancer and other health problems, procedures used to diagnose lung cancer include:

> Chest x-ray - to look for any mass or spot on the lungs.

> Sputum cytology - a study of phlegm (spit) cells under a microscope

> Needle biopsy - a needle is guided into the mass while the lungs are being viewed on a CT scan and a sample of the mass is removed and evaluated in the pathology laboratory under a microscope

> Bronchoscopy and Biopsy - a fiberoptic flexible, lighted tube is passed through the mouth into the bronchi to help find centrally located tumours or blockages, and gather samples of tissue or fluids to be examined under a microscope

> Mediastinoscopy and Biopsy -  a process in which involves a small cut through the skin at the base of the neck so that a tissue sample can be taken from the lymph nodes (mediastinal nodes) along the windpipe and the major bronchial tube areas to evaluate under a microscope
 

Tests to determine if the cancer has spread from where it started from into other areas of the body include:

 CT body scan
 Liver ultrasound scan
 Liver isotope scan
 Isotope bone scan


Treatment for lung cancer

Specific treatment is determined based on:

 Age, overall health, and medical history
 Extent of the disease
 Tolerance for specific medications, procedures, or therapies
 Expectations for the course of the disease
 Patients opinion or preference
 
 

Surgery, radiation therapy and chemotherapy may be used in the treatment of lung cancer

Surgery

Three main types of surgery are most often used in lung cancer treatment.  The choice depends on the size and location of the tumour, the extent of the cancer, the general health of the patient and some other factors.

Segmental or wedge resection - to remove only a small part of the lung

Lobectomy - removal of an entire lobe of the lung

Pneumonectomy - removal of an entire lung

Fact & Figures

National:
Lung cancer was the third most frequent cancer in men and the fifth most common cancer in women.

One in 17 men will develop lung cancer.
One in 44 women will develop lung cancer.

Lung cancer incidence for both men and women was significantly higher than expected in Dublin.  There 158 more cases of lung cancer per year in Dublin than would have been expected for the national averages.

International:
Male incidence was low by European standards.  Ireland had the third lowest incidence of the countries studies.  However, female incidence was third highest in Europe, lower than that of Denmark and the United Kingdom.

*Source:   National Cancer Registry 1995
