SLAUGHTER OF THE INNOCENT

Even though the United States are seen as the leaders of the world in every aspect they still appear to be in the Middle Age when it comes to dealing with their criminals. Since 1890 the electric chair has been used to execute criminals.

Ever since then there has been a wave of controversy in the United States. Indeed it is a grisly end but what most protestors are trying to prove is that there is too much room for error and innocent people can be executed, also another issue that has arose is that race plays a factor when the punishment is being decided.

Since 1973, a total of 69 people have been released from Death Row after their innocence had been proven or there proved to be not enough evidence that would have saved them from conviction. This is a number out of 6,000 been convicted. Which works out at 1 out of every 100. These statistics prove that the legal system in the United States has its flaws and has made mistakes known and unknown in the past and will undoubtedly continue to do so.

There are many causes to why an innocent person could be tried and found guilty of a crime. Crimes of a chilling nature, i.e. child murders, elderly people being raped, family murders, serial killers and serial rapists all receive attention in the media and public rage along with demands for arrests put severe pressure on the police. The police don’t have an easy job and along with the dangers that their jobs come with they don’t need the added pressure from an angry public. The detectives don’t do it consciously but as soon as they see any sign of potential guilt they will try and establish that the person is guilty. This establishment can also be helped by unreliable witnesses and even the actual offender themselves may help to frame and give false testimony in the court of law.

The added publicity to the cases also can influence the jury. If it is a notorious crime then it will have received a lot of publicity from the media, and before a juror may even heard any evidence in the court they may already have formed a verdict in their own mind, without being rational and listening to the evidence being presented.

The above are all factors contributing to whites and blacks being convicted. But black people have more than that to go against them. It has been proven that a black person is eleven times more likely to be convicted of killing a white person that a white person being convicted for killing a black person. About 84% of the victims in death penalty cases are white, even though only 50% of overall murder cases are the victims white.

‘In 82% of the studies reviewed, race of the victim was found to influence the likelihood of being charged with capital murder or receiving the death penalty, i.e., those who murdered whites were more likely to be sentenced to death than those who murdered blacks.’
- U.S General Accounting Office, Death Penalty Sentencing, Feb 1990.
The fact that there has been a number of people freed from death row make one think that the legal system does recognise its errors and releases the innocent before they are executed. But this isn’t always the case.

Advances in DNA have established many innocent prisoners’ innocence. In the recent case of Rolando Cruz, Alejandro Hernandez, Vernal Jimerson and Dennis Williams advanced DNA testing ensured their release. They were evidence prior to this though that they were innocent because another man had confessed to the crime and the evidence against them wasn’t very substantial. Now the four men are free and three former prosecutors and four police officers have been indicted for obstruction of justice in this case.  If their appeals had run out four or five years after their convictions, they would be dead today.

Jonathan Treadway in Arizona 1975 was freed thanks to medical science. He had been convicted for the murder and rape of a six year old girl and sentenced to death. At a retrial five pathologists testified that the victim probably died of natural causes and there was no evidence of sodomy.

Another victim of police mispractice was Jerry Banks of Georgia. He was released in 1975 after been sentenced to death for two counts of murder. The conviction was later quashed because it came out that the prosecution had withheld evidence that proved their innocence. But this action had further consequences. As a result Mr. Banks committed suicide following his divorce. His estate sued the state and won benefits for his children.

That same year in Louisiana, Johnny Ross was released when his blood type was found to be inconsistent with that of the rapist’s.

It took more than two trials before Kirk Bloodsworth’s innocence was proven in Maryland 1984. He had been convicted and sentenced for the rape and murder of a young girl. He was later granted a new trial and got a life sentence instead of execution. But his innocence was later proven when DNA testing confirmed his innocence.

Gregory R. Wilhoit wasn’t acquitted until 1993 at a retrial. In 1987 he had been convicted of killing his estranged wife. In 1991 he was released when 11 forensic experts testified that a bite mark found on the victim did not belong to him. The appeals court also found ineffective assistance of counsel. It had taken seven years.

But expert and medical testimony hasn’t always been beneficiary to defendants, Anthony Ray Peek was released in 1978 after was proven to be false.

In 1974, Joseph Green Brown of Florida was released 13 hours before his execution. The 11th Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that the prosecution had a knowingly allowed false testimony to be introduced into the trial.

Patrick Croy’s conviction in California 1979 was quashed because of improper jury instructions. His argument was changed to self - defence at the retrial.

Again in Florida 1980, Earnest Miller freed because he hadn’t been allowed introduce evidence that would have proven his innocence during trial. He was later awarded compensation from the Pasco County Sheriff’s Department because of official errors.

That same year Clarence Brandley was released in Texas. He too hadn’t been allowed produce evidence that would have proven his innocence, there was also perjury by prosecution witnesses.

Also that same year in Georgia, Gary Nelson was released after it was established that the prosecutor’s had withheld evidence from the defence.

In 1988, Walter McMillan of Alabama was freed following three witnesses withdrawing their evidence and prosecutors agreeing that the case had been mishandled.

Even though the media puts pressure on the police and jurors to make a conviction they often are responsible for having innocent prisoners released. ‘The Thin Blue Line’ was based on the convicted killer on death row Randall Dale Adams. During the initial documentary by Errol Morris the real killer confessed to his crime. This documentary resulted in the film and the release of Adams.

In the same state – Texas, Clarence Bradley had been convicted of murder and rape in a racially charged case. Civil rights groups took an interest in his case and tried to prove his innocence. Due to this publicity the American television show – ’60 Minutes’ made a documentary about Bradley. He was later released. These two men were lucky, because not all death row innocents have media attention or finance to get them back into the appeals courts.

It is evident that there is a growing rise in the number of wrongful murder convictions with a sentence of execution. In contrast the opportunity to appeal and to produce new evidence proving evidence has decreased. The government funding for the death penalty resource centres that help prove innocence and provide financial aid has been withdrawn. Some courts now believe it is acceptable to go ahead and hand out the death penalty when there is reasonable doubt to the guilt of a defendant.
