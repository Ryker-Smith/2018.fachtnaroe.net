History and Structure of the Internet
By Sonia Bonelli

The Internet

The Internet has become a vital part of communication today. More and more businesses are using it to expand as with over 150,000,000 potential customers they have the prospect of global business opportunities and profit. It is becoming a major part of the consumer industry. The Internet is basically a network of computer networks, a network being ‘An interconnection collection of computers’. If two computers can exchange information they are interconnected. A network is a group of computers connected by a communications link that enables any device to interact with another on the network.

History

The Internet grew from defense and research requirements in the USA in the 1960’s. It was designed to withstand failure even from nuclear attack. It has survived and prospered to today as it was found to be a very easy way to communicate and do business. In 1969, it began as a network of 4 computers called the ‘ARPANET’, and so, the Internet was off the ground. For the first decade, it was primarily used for electronic mail and to help transfer files from the government agencies, companies and universities.

In 1973, the US Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) started a research program to see if they could interlink networks of various kinds. The objective was to develop a way in which networked computers could communicate across linked packet networks. The project and the system of networks that emerged from this research became known as the ‘Internet’. By 1983, 200 computers were linked across the USA.

During the 1980’s, Internet protocols were introduced. A protocol is a series of rules for communication between two computers, that everyone agrees to, and they govern format, timing and error control. The Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) and the Internet Protocol became the only set of protocols used on the Internet. The government needed to separate military and non-military sites as the Internet (as it became known) was growing and so, it was divided in two ‘MILNET’ and ‘ARPANET’. By 1985, the conversion of the TCP/IP was now completed and the Internet came together.

In 1986, the National Science Foundation (NSF) connected six super computer centers. These provide a communication service and are a major backbone of the Internet today. NASA and the U.S. Department of Energy contributed additional facilities in connecting to the Internet, and in Europe ‘NORDUNET’ and other international companies provide connection to thousands of computers on a large number of networks.

The 1990’s introduced the HyperText Markup Language (HTML) protocol. Now individuals could create pages on the Internet or ‘web sites’ using this computer language. The Internet thus became public and is now known as the ‘World Wide Web’, or ‘www’ for short, which grew from an idea by Englishman Tim Berners Lee. A protocol called Open System Interconnection (OSI) was available by 1991 and the Internet had grown to 4,000,000 users. In 1995, the Internet became public and there were 5,000,000 users. Today, the Internet has 43,000,000 users but this is growing, as the Internet is becoming more popular.

The Structure of the Internet

The Internet is controlled by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) and no single body owns it. The IETF are working groups in different parts of the world who discuss specific issues in relation to the Internet. This makes it decentralised in nature as anyone can write pages on it or provide information on it with little control. All standards for the Internet are open and publicly available as propriety solutions are a threat to these standards. Because it is a global organisation change in protocols occur more slowly, but they do occur as the Internet is always changing.

The Internet works with a combination and technologies and protocols. These allow the computers on the Internet to communicate e.g. TCP/IP protocols. There are different ways to carry the information from computer to computer:

Telephone wire

Cable TV wire

Ethernet wire

High speed fiber optics

Cellular transmissions

Satellite transmissions

But connection requires hardware as in a computer, modem and a phone line. Software as in TCP/IP, a browser e.g. Netscape and electronic mail are also needed. And an Internet Service Provider (ISP) is also needed. An ISP sells Internet access and helps customers get connected. Examples of ISP’s in Ireland are Eircom.net, indigo.ie and iol.ie. A modem is a box that connects the user’s computer to the Internet.

Electronic mail (E-mail) is one of the main applications on the Internet. E-mail has the same rules as letter writing. The growth of the Internet is partly due to the ability to send ‘letters’ from computer to computer. ISP’s usually provide a standard e-mail address and mail can be checked on any computer. It is paid for with advertising.

There has been growing concern on the Internet about security. But there are a lot of means to secure and keep confidentiality. Passwords, firewalls and filters are all examples of securing a network. Viruses are also a concern but these can be prevented using anti-viral software. Other security measures such as revealing personal details with care and only purchasing with credit card can be used also.

Addresses for the Internet are called URL’s. URL’s are shown across a bar on the top of the computer screen. A URL specifies the protocol, server location and file name e.g. http://www.yahoo.com/index.html. A server is used as back up to secure and gain access to files on a network.
