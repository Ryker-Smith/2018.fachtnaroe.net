Is The Internet a Democracy:


Yes the Internet is a democracy. The people who choose to use the Internet help in how it works. Anyone can go on the 'net' and exchange idea's with people about anything without the feeling of government filtering or commercial partiality. This is why most users of the Internet are against censorship or control of information on the Internet. Further sometimes people like to chat without anyone knowing what they look like or their race or sex.


It is seen that democracy is a privilege of cyberspace, the only real form of mass communications. The Internet is seen as any-to-any where by it doesn't matter the place or the race. The only problem here is that the any-to-any connectivity can only occur when the acquired hardware is installed in the persons own machine that they are working from. People then need to invest in this hardware to communicate via the Internet this making the network of networks to work.


In a time where people seem to be less concerned about their democratic right to be involved in society the Internet seems to be one of the reason's for this even though it is a gateway for people to get involved if they choose to do so. Something that has eluded the people in power to date. The Internet compares to a company in the respect's that both money and know how is needed to make it work, yet differs from a company in the respects that there is no chairman like figure to control it.


Which brings me to my next point Who Owns The Internet? And the answer to that is no-one and no single person or organisation control it. The Internet relies on a physical infrastructure that connects networks to other networks. There are many different organisations, corporations, governments, schools, private citizens and service providers that all own pieces of the infrastructure but there is no one body that owns it all. There are however organisations that oversee and standardise what happens on the Internet ans assign IP address's and domain names such as the National Science Foundation, the Internet Engineering Task Force, ICANN, InterNIC and the Internet Architecture Board. These in turn all make the Internet work.


How Does The Internet Work? Different parts of the Internet work differently but they all use the main network and transmit information the same way. Information travels around the Internet in packets and if the packet is too big it is broken down and reassembled at it's destination. As the packets travel to their destination they bounce from peer to peer like pinballs. They are directed around the Internet by routers which the determine the best and fastest route around the Internet for each packet. Physical distance means nothing in cyberspace.


Where did The Internet Come From? The Internet was developed in 1970 by ARPAnet, the Advanced Research Projects of the U.S. Department of Defense. It was designed to allow the scientific community to communicate. It was reportedly created specifically to survive a nuclear attack that would have cut off normal communications, like telephones, because it is self healing. That means if one part is shutdown, the routers reroute the traffic around the shutdown. It fixes itself. In its early years, before the development of the World Wide Web, academic and scientific institutions monopolised the Internet. It's a good thing too. No one other that someone with an advanced computer engineering degree could find anything on the Internet before the Web was developed.
