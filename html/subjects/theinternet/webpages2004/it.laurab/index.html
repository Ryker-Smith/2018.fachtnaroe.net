Where Did The Internet Come From?


Most people see the Internet as a means of communication, a quick and easy way to talk to friends and family in such far off places as the U.S. and Australia. The U.S. Defence Department originally had this intent in mind when they connected a computer network with various other radio and satellite networks. They wanted a means of communicating within the U.S. military that could withstand partial attacks, such as bomb attacks and still function.


In August 1962, a researcher at MIT by the name of J.C.R Licklider wrote a series of memos that outlined a “Galactic Network”of interconnected computers whereby everyone could quickly access informations and programs from any site. Another researcher at MIT, Leonard Kleinrock, published a paper in July 1961 that would make communication on the Internet more feasible. Kleinrock's paper on the packet switching theory convinced MIT researcher Lawrence Roberts to set up an experiment that involved connecting a TX-2 computer at MIT to a AN/FSQ-32 computer at System Development Corp. at Santa Monica, California. This experiment resulted in the first computer network ever built. In 1966 Roberts took his computer network expertise to the U.S. Defence Advanced Research Projects Agency(APRA) and quickly put together a plan for “ARPANET” . During the implementation of this plan, Leonard Kleinrock's theory of packet switching was put into practice by the developing of key components called Interface Message Processors(IMPs).


The Impact of this development led to the assembly of the APRANET. The first site chosen to implement the ARPANET was the Network Measurement Center at UCLA. This was made possible by the installation of the first IMP and the first host computer at UCLA in September 1969.Then later the Stanford Research Institute, the University of Utah were added to the beginning of the ARPANET. In October 1972, the ARPANET was successfully demonstrated at the International Computer Communication Conference. It is this ARPANET that grew into what we know as the Internet.


Two different developments came form ARPANET: the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP/IP) and e-mail. These developments laid the ground work for the commercialisation of Internet technology.


Where Did The World Wide Web Come From?


In 1980, a British computer scientist named Tim Berners-Lee was working at a Swiss physics laboratory when he got fed up with his computerised daily schedule planner. The phone numbers he saved and the documents he wrote were stored on separate databases and there was no easy way to connect them. He created a hypertext computer program that allowed him to make connections. Later, he expanded his idea outside the office and developed a a program that could link databases around the world. He called his idea the “World Wide Web”. When he distributed the software in 1991 other computer scientist's started building on it. Eventually, a group of students at the University if Illinois came up with a program called Mosaic. It was the first web browser, a tool that turned Berners-Lee's textual world into a graphical, point-and-click wonderland. Some of the people who developed Mosaic went on to invent Netscape Navigater.



Who Owns The Internet?


No one actually owns the Internet, and no single person or organisation controls the Internet in its entirety. The Internet relies on a physical infrastructure that connects networks to other networks. There are many organisations, corporations, governments, schools, private citizens that all own pieces of the infrastructure, but there is no body that owns it. There are, however, organisations that oversee and standardise what happens on the Internet and assign IP addresses and domain names.
